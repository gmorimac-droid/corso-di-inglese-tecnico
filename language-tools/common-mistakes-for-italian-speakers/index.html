    Common Mistakes for Italian Speakers¶

    Purpose of this page¶

    Italian-speaking learners of technical English often possess strong technical reasoning and adequate specialist vocabulary, yet still produce writing that sounds translated rather than naturally professional. The problem is not usually lack of intelligence or lack of subject knowledge. It is often a question of transfer: Italian sentence structure, collocation patterns, and rhetorical habits are carried directly into English technical prose.

    This page identifies the most recurrent patterns and shows how to correct them in a way that supports engineering clarity, not only grammatical accuracy.

    1. Translating sentence rhythm directly from Italian¶

    Italian technical writing often tolerates longer sentence chains and more embedded explanation than English documentation normally prefers. English technical prose usually benefits from shorter clauses, clearer segmentation, and stronger sentence hierarchy.

    Weaker version¶

    The component, which is installed in the upper part of the frame and that has the function to support the connector, is very important for the stability of the system.

    Stronger version¶

    The component is installed in the upper part of the frame and supports the connector. Its primary role is to improve system stability.

    Correction principle¶

    Do not try to reproduce Italian sentence length. Write in smaller logical units.

    2. Using generic adjectives instead of technical meaning¶

    Italian learners often rely on words such as good, bad, important, critical, or strong when a more precise technical descriptor would communicate more effectively.

    Weak¶

    The connector was in a bad condition.

    Better¶

    The connector showed local contamination and reduced retention.

    Stronger technical options¶

    • damaged
    • deformed
    • misaligned
    • contaminated
    • serviceable
    • non-compliant
    • within tolerance
    • structurally intact

    3. Overusing “must” where “shall” or “should” is more appropriate¶

    Many Italian learners use must for all strong statements. In professional technical English, the choice between shall, should, and must often depends on document type.

    • shall: formal requirement
    • should: recommendation or good practice
    • must: strong obligation, often used in procedures or safety-critical statements

    Example¶

    • The bracket shall withstand the specified load.
    • A follow-up inspection should be performed after transport.
    • Personnel must not energise the unit while the cover is removed.

    4. Confusing “actual” and “attuale”¶

    This is one of the most persistent false friends.

    • English actual = real / effettivo
    • Italian attuale usually corresponds to current in English

    Incorrect¶

    The actual configuration includes revision 3.

    Correct¶

    The current configuration includes revision 3.

    5. Writing “make a control” instead of “perform a check”¶

    Many direct translations from Italian sound unnatural because they use a generic verb plus noun combination that does not exist in normal English technical collocation.

    Prefer these forms¶

    • perform a check
    • conduct an inspection
    • carry out verification
    • issue a report
    • release a document
    • record a value
    • submit a revision

    6. Using “control” when the real meaning is “check” or “inspect”¶

    In English technical contexts, control often means regulation, command, or active management, not simple verification.

    Compare¶

    • The control unit regulates the output signal.
    • The operator checked the connector alignment.

    Common correction¶

    Italian controllare may correspond to: - check - inspect - verify - review - monitor - control only when actual regulation or command is involved

    7. Translating “to support” too widely¶

    Italian writers sometimes use support for every kind of physical or functional help. In English technical writing, verb choice should reflect the exact type of support.

    Possible alternatives¶

    • support for structural or organisational support
    • withstand for mechanical resistance or tolerance
    • enable for functional possibility
    • facilitate for process improvement
    • sustain in some specialised contexts

    Example¶

    • weak: The material supports high temperature.
    • better: The material withstands high temperature exposure.

    8. Overusing “important”¶

    Important is not wrong, but it is often weak in technical prose.

    Weak¶

    This parameter is important for performance.

    Better¶

    This parameter is critical to thermal stability.

    Even better when possible¶

    This parameter directly affects thermal stability and response time.

    9. Incorrect use of prepositions¶

    Prepositions are a persistent error area because direct transfer from Italian is very common.

    Frequent corrections¶

    • compliant with, not compliant to
    • related to, not usually related with
    • responsible for, not responsible of
    • dependent on, not dependent from
    • consistent with, not consistent to
    • according to, not in according with

    10. Using “eventually” for “eventualmente”¶

    In English, eventually means in the end or after some time. It does not usually mean if needed or possibly.

    Incorrect¶

    Eventually, an additional test can be done.

    Correct depending on meaning¶

    • If necessary, an additional test can be performed.
    • A further test may be performed if required.

    11. Translating “sensible” from “sensibile”¶

    • English sensible = reasonable, practical
    • English sensitive = sensitive, responsive, delicate

    Example¶

    The sensor is highly sensitive to pressure variation.

    12. Unnatural use of “function” and “have the function to”¶

    A very common transfer pattern is has the function to. This is grammatically understandable but stylistically weak.

    Prefer¶

    • The function of the valve is to regulate flow.
    • The valve regulates flow.
    • The bracket is intended to support the assembly.

    13. Overusing the passive with weak subject control¶

    Italian learners sometimes produce passive-heavy English where the actor remains unclear and the sentence becomes difficult to follow.

    Weak¶

    It was decided that the document was to be updated after it was reviewed.

    Better¶

    The review team decided to update the document after completing the technical review.

    Use passive voice, but do not use it as a substitute for structure.

    14. Writing “is composed by” instead of “is composed of”¶

    Correct forms¶

    • The assembly is composed of three sections.
    • The system consists of three sections.
    • The unit includes a power stage and a control module.

    15. False equivalence between “problem” and technical issue types¶

    Italian problema is broad. In English technical writing, more specific nouns are often better.

    Alternatives¶

    • issue
    • fault
    • failure
    • deviation
    • non-conformity
    • anomaly
    • malfunction
    • constraint

    Example¶

    An intermittent communication fault was detected during the startup sequence.

    16. Translating emphasis instead of evidence¶

    Italian technical speech may sometimes tolerate stronger evaluative emphasis. English engineering prose usually prefers evidence-based qualification.

    Weak¶

    The result was very good and absolutely acceptable.

    Better¶

    The result remained within the specified tolerance range and met the acceptance criteria.

    17. Using “before to” instead of “before + -ing / noun / clause”¶

    Incorrect¶

    Before to start the test, inspect the connector.

    Correct¶

    • Before starting the test, inspect the connector.
    • Before the test begins, inspect the connector.
    • Before test execution, inspect the connector.

    18. Using “informations” and other non-count forms incorrectly¶

    Many technical nouns that are plural in Italian remain uncountable in English.

    Common examples¶

    • information not informations
    • equipment not equipments
    • advice not advices
    • evidence often uncountable in technical reporting

    19. Weak paragraph logic¶

    A frequent B2-level issue is not individual grammar, but paragraph organisation. Learners mix observation, interpretation, action, and conclusion without signalling the shift.

    Better paragraph sequence¶

    1. observation;
    2. supporting data;
    3. interpretation;
    4. action taken;
    5. residual concern or next step.

    20. Revision checklist for Italian-speaking learners¶

    Before releasing a text, ask:

    • Did I translate structure directly from Italian?
    • Is any sentence too long for its function?
    • Did I choose a vague adjective where a technical descriptor is available?
    • Are my modals aligned with the document type?
    • Are my prepositions natural in English?
    • Did I use actual, eventually, sensible, or control correctly?
    • Are observation and interpretation separated clearly?

    Final note¶

    Do not translate professionalism literally

    Professional technical English is not obtained by translating Italian word for word at a high level of formality. It is obtained by choosing the wording that makes the engineering meaning precise, reviewable, and proportionate.
