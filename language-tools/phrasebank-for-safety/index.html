    Phrasebank for Safety¶

    Purpose of this page¶

    Safety language in technical English must be precise, proportionate, and operationally clear. Vague safety statements are dangerous because they create the appearance of caution without providing usable guidance. In engineering, maintenance, inspection, and compliance documentation, safety wording must identify hazards, conditions, consequences, and mitigations in a way that supports action and review.

    This phrasebank provides reusable patterns for warnings, restrictions, mitigation, incident summaries, and escalation.

    1. Warning and prohibition language¶

    Core phrases¶

    • The unit shall not be energised while the connector remains exposed.
    • Operation shall be suspended if damage to the sealing surface is detected.
    • The procedure shall not continue unless continuity has been verified.
    • Unauthorised bypass of the verification step is prohibited.
    • Personnel shall not remove the protective cover during powered operation.
    • The item shall not be released to service until the non-conformity has been closed.

    Usage note¶

    Prohibition language should define clearly what is not allowed and, where relevant, under what condition the prohibition applies.

    2. Hazard identification language¶

    Core phrases¶

    • The identified hazard is associated with exposure to rotating parts.
    • The principal risk relates to unintended energisation during maintenance activity.
    • This condition introduces a risk of contamination ingress at the interface.
    • The configuration creates a potential pinch hazard during closure.
    • The observed damage may reduce environmental protection capability.

    3. Risk and consequence language¶

    Core phrases¶

    • Failure to install the protective cap may result in contamination ingress.
    • This condition increases the risk of interface degradation.
    • Continued operation in this state may lead to loss of signal integrity.
    • Improper torque application may result in mechanical loosening during service.
    • If the condition is not corrected, progressive wear may occur.

    Usage note¶

    Use may, can, or is likely to when the consequence is plausible but not strictly deterministic. Use stronger wording only when the causal relationship is established and the risk statement must be categorical.

    4. Mitigation and preventive action¶

    Core phrases¶

    • To reduce the risk of damage, install the protective cover before transport.
    • Immediate cleaning and inspection are required following contamination exposure.
    • Additional verification shall be performed before release to service.
    • Escalation is required if the condition cannot be resolved locally.
    • A follow-up inspection should be scheduled after the next operating cycle.
    • The affected item shall be quarantined pending technical review.

    5. Procedural safety language¶

    Core phrases¶

    • Prior to maintenance, isolate the unit from all power sources.
    • Before opening the enclosure, verify that the system is in a safe condition.
    • After reassembly, confirm closure integrity before re-energisation.
    • Use approved handling equipment when moving the assembly.
    • Record any abnormal condition before returning the item to service.

    6. Safety-oriented recommendation language¶

    Core phrases¶

    • A more explicit warning statement is recommended in the procedure.
    • Additional visual marking should be considered for the access panel.
    • The current instruction would benefit from a clearer isolation step.
    • A short operator note is recommended to reduce misuse risk.

    7. Incident summary language¶

    Core phrases¶

    • The event occurred during nominal startup sequencing.
    • No injury was reported; however, the incident triggered formal technical review.
    • The immediate response consisted of isolation, inspection, and documentation.
    • Further preventive action has been recommended to reduce recurrence.
    • The condition was identified before release to service.
    • The event did not result in equipment loss, but it exposed a procedural weakness.

    8. Escalation and residual risk language¶

    Core phrases¶

    • The risk remains open until verification evidence has been completed.
    • The condition requires formal review before operational release.
    • Residual concern remains regarding long-term durability under repeated exposure.
    • The current controls are considered sufficient for interim use only.
    • Further action is required before the risk can be considered reduced to an acceptable level.

    9. Safety paragraph models¶

    Model A: warning note¶

    The enclosure shall not be opened while the unit is energised. Failure to isolate the system before maintenance may result in exposure to live electrical interfaces. Prior to access, verify that all power sources have been disconnected and that the safe condition has been confirmed.

    Model B: mitigation note¶

    Local contamination was observed on the connector sealing surface. To reduce the risk of moisture ingress, the affected area shall be cleaned and re-inspected before reassembly. If damage to the seal is confirmed, the connector shall be replaced prior to further operation.

    Model C: incident summary¶

    During nominal startup sequencing, an abnormal condition was identified at the external interface. No injury occurred, and the system was isolated immediately. The event triggered inspection, documentation, and review of the associated maintenance instruction. Preventive action has been recommended to reduce recurrence.

    10. Weak vs strong safety wording¶

    Weak¶

    Be careful with the connector because it could be dangerous.

    Stronger¶

    The connector shall not be handled while the unit is energised. Exposure to the live interface may result in electrical hazard.

    Weak¶

    It is better to check the seal before use.

    Stronger¶

    Prior to operation, inspect the seal for visible damage or contamination. Operation shall not continue if sealing integrity cannot be confirmed.

    11. Review checklist for safety language¶

    • Does the statement identify the hazard clearly?
    • Is the prohibited action explicit?
    • Is the consequence credible and proportionate?
    • Is the mitigation operationally clear?
    • Does the sentence distinguish between requirement and recommendation?

    Final note¶

    Safety language must be actionable

    A strong safety statement tells the reader what condition matters, what must not happen, what action is required, and what consequence may follow if the instruction is ignored.
