    Technical Adjectives and Descriptors¶

    Scope of this glossary

    This glossary focuses on adjectives and descriptive terms that strengthen technical writing by making it more precise and less conversational. Many B2 learners know the underlying concepts but still rely on weak wording such as good, strong, bad, or useful. The descriptors below provide more professional alternatives.

    How to read and reuse the entries¶

    Each entry is written for active professional use. The definition gives conceptual precision, the example shows sentence-level application, and the usage note explains where the term is especially valuable in engineering communication. Where helpful, a contrast note is added to prevent common learner mistakes.

    Glossary entries¶

    robust¶

    Definition. Robust describes a design, process, or system that continues to function effectively under variable, demanding, or imperfect conditions.

    Example. The revised connector retention concept proved more robust under repeated handling and vibration exposure.

    Usage note. Useful in design comparison, review commentary, and performance summaries.

    Contrast / learner note. Robust does not mean heavy or oversized. It refers to dependable performance under challenge.

    Related terms. resilient, reliable, durable, tolerant

    reliable¶

    Definition. Reliable describes something that performs its intended function consistently over time and under stated conditions.

    Example. The architecture is reliable provided that power quality remains within the specified limits.

    Usage note. A strong adjective for reports and recommendations, but it should ideally be supported by evidence.

    Contrast / learner note. Reliable is not the same as available or maintainable, although the concepts are related.

    Related terms. dependable, stable, consistent, maintainable

    redundant¶

    Definition. Redundant describes an additional element or path included so that the function can continue if one element fails or becomes unavailable.

    Example. The communication chain includes a redundant data path to preserve status reporting in the event of a single-point fault.

    Usage note. Use this term in system architecture, safety-related design reasoning, and reliability discussions.

    Contrast / learner note. In everyday English, redundant can mean unnecessary. In engineering, it often means intentionally duplicated for resilience.

    Related terms. backup, fail-safe, resilience, availability

    scalable¶

    Definition. Scalable describes a design or architecture that can be expanded, adapted, or increased in capacity without major redesign.

    Example. The software architecture is scalable enough to support additional sensing channels with limited reconfiguration effort.

    Usage note. Common in digital systems, platforms, data processing, and modular product families.

    Contrast / learner note. Scalable does not necessarily mean currently large. It means capable of efficient growth.

    Related terms. modular, extensible, configurable, adaptable

    modular¶

    Definition. Modular describes a design composed of separate functional units that can be combined, replaced, or upgraded with limited disruption to the whole system.

    Example. A modular enclosure concept simplified maintenance access and reduced replacement time for failed units.

    Usage note. Useful in architecture discussions, supportability arguments, and product-family descriptions.

    Contrast / learner note. Modular design supports change, but it does not automatically guarantee standardisation or interoperability.

    Related terms. scalable, replaceable, supportable, configurable

    compliant¶

    Definition. Compliant describes an item, design, process, or document that satisfies stated requirements, rules, standards, or criteria.

    Example. The revised report is compliant with the current document template and review checklist.

    Usage note. A useful adjective when the subject has already been assessed against an explicit reference set.

    Contrast / learner note. Do not use compliant without implying or stating the reference against which compliance was judged.

    Related terms. conformant, acceptable, verified, approved

    portable¶

    Definition. Portable describes equipment or a solution that can be moved, carried, or relocated without excessive difficulty and without unacceptable setup burden.

    Example. The diagnostic tool is portable enough for use in both workshop and field-support environments.

    Usage note. Useful in product descriptions and support discussions.

    Contrast / learner note. Portable is not the same as handheld. Portable equipment may still require transport cases or setup support.

    Related terms. lightweight, deployable, field-capable, mobile

    maintainable¶

    Definition. Maintainable describes a system or design that can be inspected, serviced, repaired, or restored efficiently and safely.

    Example. The revised cable routing is more maintainable because the inspection points remain accessible without major disassembly.

    Usage note. Very valuable in design reviews and supportability arguments.

    Contrast / learner note. Maintainable concerns ease of maintenance, not only maintenance frequency.

    Related terms. supportable, accessible, serviceable, lifecycle-efficient

    interoperable¶

    Definition. Interoperable describes systems or elements that can work together effectively through compatible data, procedures, and interfaces.

    Example. The selected message format is interoperable with the wider monitoring environment and existing archive tools.

    Usage note. Common in systems engineering and multi-party environments.

    Contrast / learner note. Interoperable is broader than electrically compatible or physically connectable.

    Related terms. compatible, integrated, coordinated, standardised

    configurable¶

    Definition. Configurable describes a system or product that can be adjusted or tailored through defined settings or controlled options.

    Example. The alarm thresholds are configurable within an approved range to support different operating profiles.

    Usage note. Useful in software, instrumentation, and adaptable hardware platforms.

    Contrast / learner note. Configurable does not mean uncontrolled or user-modifiable in any way. Good technical writing should indicate limits and permissions.

    Related terms. adjustable, selectable, parameterised, scalable

    lightweight¶

    Definition. Lightweight describes a component, structure, or solution with relatively low mass, often as a design objective linked to transportability or integration limits.

    Example. A lightweight bracket was selected to reduce total system mass without compromising stiffness.

    Usage note. Use this term carefully and preferably with context such as structural constraints, portability, or platform limits.

    Contrast / learner note. Lightweight should not imply fragility unless evidence supports that concern.

    Related terms. portable, low-mass, compact, efficient

    ruggedised¶

    Definition. Ruggedised describes equipment deliberately adapted or designed to tolerate harsh environmental or handling conditions.

    Example. The field laptop uses a ruggedised enclosure to improve durability during transport and outdoor operation.

    Usage note. Common in support, field-use, and environmental qualification contexts.

    Contrast / learner note. Because the term is broad, good writing should indicate what conditions the item is ruggedised against.

    Related terms. robust, hardened, protected, resilient

    Weak versus strong description¶

    Weak description often relies on vague adjectives such as good, strong, bad, or suitable. Strong technical description uses controlled adjectives connected to a reason or condition, for example maintainable because access points remain visible or robust under repeated vibration and handling.

    Writing patterns worth reusing¶

    The value of a glossary increases when the learner converts terminology into reusable sentence patterns. After studying the entries above, build short patterns such as:

    • The system shall ... under the specified operating conditions.
    • The inspection confirmed that ...
    • The interface between X and Y is responsible for ...
    • A deviation was recorded because ...
    • The revised document includes ...

    Suggested micro-practice¶

    1. Select five entries from this page and write one sentence for each in a different document genre.
    2. Rewrite one weak sentence by replacing generic vocabulary with glossary terms.
    3. Add one contrast pair to your personal notes, for example verification vs validation or incident vs near miss.
    4. Reuse at least three entries in a module assignment before moving to the next glossary.
