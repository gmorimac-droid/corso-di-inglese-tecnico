# Safety Exercises

## Purpose

Safety-related communication in engineering must be clear, controlled, and proportionate. It should identify hazards, state consequences, define required action, and avoid both understatement and unnecessary alarmism. This page develops the language needed for warnings, cautions, risk statements, and incident communication.

---

## Exercise 1 — Hazard, Risk, or Control?

Classify each item below as:
- hazard;
- risk;
- control measure;
- consequence.

### Items
1. exposed energized conductors  
2. electric shock during enclosure opening  
3. isolate power before maintenance  
4. injury to maintenance personnel  
5. inadequate cable retention  
6. abrasion during routine handling  
7. protective sleeve installation

### Follow-Up
Explain the relationship between hazard, risk, and control measure in one short paragraph.

---

## Exercise 2 — Warning vs Caution

Rewrite the following statements as either a **Warning** or a **Caution**, depending on severity.

1. Opening the enclosure while powered may expose personnel to live conductors.
2. Excessive torque may damage the mounting threads.
3. Improper cable routing may lead to progressive abrasion.
4. Contact with hot external surfaces may occur after extended operation.

### Reviewer Focus
The learner must match the message level to the consequence.

---

## Exercise 3 — Safety Note Improvement

Improve the note below.

> Be careful when using the system because some parts may be dangerous.

### Tasks
1. explain why the sentence is weak;
2. rewrite it as a technically credible safety statement;
3. specify the hazard and consequence.

---

## Exercise 4 — Risk Statement Writing

Write a short risk statement for each situation:
1. use of the unit outside the defined temperature range;
2. incomplete connector engagement;
3. missing documentation after maintenance;
4. incorrect lifting of heavy enclosure;
5. bypassing inspection step before release.

### Output Requirement
Each statement should include:
- condition;
- possible consequence;
- implied or explicit control.

---

## Exercise 5 — Incident Summary

Write an incident summary based on the following:
- technician opened enclosure before verifying isolation;
- no injury occurred;
- procedural non-compliance identified;
- immediate briefing conducted;
- additional checklist step introduced.

### Tone
Formal, factual, improvement-oriented.

---

## Exercise 6 — Safety Communication for Operators

Write a short operator-facing safety instruction related to:
- thermal hazard;
- moving part hazard;
- electrical isolation;
- environmental limitation.

Choose one topic and write 80 to 120 words.

---

## Exercise 7 — Stronger Safety Language

Improve the following expressions:
1. use carefully  
2. avoid problems  
3. hot part  
4. dangerous cable  
5. bad condition  

### Task
Replace each with wording more suitable for formal safety documentation.

---

## Exercise 8 — Safety Review Exercise

Read the sentence and identify missing information.

> Do not install the unit incorrectly.

### Questions
- What makes this instruction weak?
- What information is missing?
- How would you rewrite it in a useful professional form?

---

## Exercise 9 — Safety and Documentation Link

Write a short paragraph explaining why safety communication must be linked to documentation control, revision status, and training.

### Reviewer Focus
The answer should go beyond generic statements and reflect engineering governance.

---

## Exercise 10 — Repository-Ready Safety Box

Write a documentation-ready safety box that could later be inserted into a module or template page. Choose one of the following:
- power isolation;
- hot surface hazard;
- vibration-related loosening;
- improper lifting;
- cable abrasion risk.

### Output Goal
Use a heading such as **Warning**, **Caution**, or **Safety Note** and write 70 to 120 words.
