?# Maintenance and Troubleshooting Exercises

## Purpose

Maintenance and troubleshooting communication requires clarity, sequence control, and evidence-based reasoning. The engineer or technician must explain what was observed, what was checked, what was found, what action was taken, and what remains open. This page develops the language needed to communicate faults and maintenance actions professionally.

---

## Exercise 1 — Symptom, Cause, Action

For each item below, identify:
- the symptom;
- the possible cause;
- a plausible corrective action.

### Items
1. intermittent loss of communication;
2. abnormal wear on cable guide;
3. repeated alignment difficulty during installation;
4. elevated surface temperature on enclosure;
5. delayed actuator response at upper temperature limit.

### Follow-Up
Write one short diagnostic sentence for each case.

---

## Exercise 2 — Sequencing a Troubleshooting Narrative

Reorder the following elements into a logical troubleshooting paragraph:
- communication restored after re-securing connector;
- intermittent data loss observed;
- initial power check confirmed nominal supply;
- loose connector identified at interface panel;
- no abnormal temperature increase detected.

### Output Goal
Write a coherent fault narrative of 90 to 130 words.

---

## Exercise 3 — Maintenance Log Entry

Write a maintenance log entry using the information below:
- date not required;
- scheduled inspection;
- cable support bracket showed wear;
- bracket replaced;
- alignment verified;
- function check passed;
- next inspection recommended after 100 operating hours.

### Tone
Concise, traceable, professional.

---

## Exercise 4 — Troubleshooting Questions

For each situation, write three troubleshooting questions that a maintenance engineer should ask.

### Situations
1. actuator response slower than expected;
2. event log not synchronizing with workstation;
3. abnormal vibration detected during operation;
4. display interface intermittently blank.

### Reviewer Focus
The questions should be diagnostic, not generic.

---

## Exercise 5 — Root Cause Discussion

Write a short root cause discussion based on the following:
- repeated loosening of connector after vibration exposure;
- connector itself not defective;
- retention method likely insufficient;
- redesign of retention bracket recommended.

### Output Requirement
Write 100 to 140 words using analytical language.

---

## Exercise 6 — Corrective vs Preventive Action

Distinguish between corrective and preventive action for the following cases:
1. loose connector found during testing;
2. wear observed on cable routing support;
3. incomplete installation record identified;
4. repeated misalignment during assembly.

### Task
For each case, write:
- one corrective action;
- one preventive action.

---

## Exercise 7 — Maintenance Procedure Writing

Write a short maintenance procedure for replacing a damaged connector.

### Required Steps
- isolate power;
- access panel removal;
- connector removal;
- inspection of mating surfaces;
- installation of replacement connector;
- alignment check;
- function verification;
- documentation update.

### Output Style
Use controlled procedural English.

---

## Exercise 8 — Troubleshooting Table

Create a troubleshooting table with four columns:
- symptom;
- probable cause;
- diagnostic action;
- corrective action.

Populate the table with at least five realistic entries.

---

## Exercise 9 — Review a Weak Maintenance Note

Improve the note below.

> The unit had a problem and we checked it. A connector was not okay, so we fixed it. Then it worked again.

### Tasks
1. identify weak wording;
2. rewrite as a professional maintenance note;
3. add one recommendation for future inspection.

---

## Exercise 10 — Applied Maintenance Case

Write a short case study of 180 to 250 words describing a maintenance intervention involving:
- detected anomaly;
- inspection process;
- identified cause;
- corrective action;
- verification result;
- recommendation for future monitoring.

### Output Goal
The final text should be strong enough to be reused later as:
- a module example;
- a model answer;
- a troubleshooting template example.
