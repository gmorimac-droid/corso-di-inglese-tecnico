# Defence and Dual-Use Terms

!!! abstract "Scope of this glossary"
    This glossary introduces professional terminology often encountered in engineering environments that support defence, security, resilience, surveillance, support, or dual-use technology programmes. The emphasis remains descriptive, institutional, and documentation-oriented. The aim is to support neutral professional English rather than sensitive operational detail.

## How to read and reuse the entries

Each entry is written for **active professional use**. The definition gives conceptual precision, the example shows sentence-level application, and the usage note explains where the term is especially valuable in engineering communication. Where helpful, a contrast note is added to prevent common learner mistakes.

## Glossary entries

### platform

**Definition.** A platform is the physical or functional base that carries, supports, or hosts systems, subsystems, payloads, or mission-related equipment.

**Example.** The sensor package was adapted for integration on a lightweight mobile platform with strict mass limits.

**Usage note.** Use this term in architecture summaries, capability descriptions, and high-level system documentation.

**Contrast / learner note.** A platform is broader than a vehicle or structure alone because it often implies an integration context.

**Related terms.** host system, payload, interface, mission profile

### payload

**Definition.** A payload is the mission-related or function-specific equipment carried by a platform in order to perform a defined role.

**Example.** The electro-optical payload required vibration isolation to preserve image stability during transit and operation.

**Usage note.** This term is useful in system descriptions where the carried capability is distinct from the platform itself.

**Contrast / learner note.** Payload is not a synonym for cargo in technical programme language. It usually refers to function-bearing equipment.

**Related terms.** platform, sensor package, mission equipment, integration

### mission profile

**Definition.** A mission profile is the structured description of expected operating phases, conditions, durations, and task demands relevant to a system or capability.

**Example.** Battery sizing was reviewed against the updated mission profile, which included longer surveillance periods and extended standby time.

**Usage note.** Use this term when performance, endurance, environmental conditions, or support concepts depend on real use patterns.

**Contrast / learner note.** A mission profile is broader than a test scenario because it reflects intended use, not only a laboratory event.

**Related terms.** operational concept, duty cycle, endurance, deployment

### readiness

**Definition.** Readiness is the state of being prepared for intended use, deployment, or task execution, considering technical condition, support status, and personnel factors where relevant.

**Example.** Reduced spare-part availability affected fleet readiness despite acceptable hardware reliability.

**Usage note.** Useful in supportability, maintenance, logistics, and programme review discussions.

**Contrast / learner note.** Readiness is not identical to reliability. A system may be technically reliable but not ready if documentation, spares, or trained personnel are missing.

**Related terms.** availability, supportability, sustainment, serviceability

### interoperability

**Definition.** Interoperability is the ability of systems, teams, or organisations to operate together effectively through compatible interfaces, procedures, data formats, and expectations.

**Example.** Interoperability requirements drove the selection of standardised communication interfaces and message structures.

**Usage note.** Common in system-of-systems work, joint programmes, integration documents, and institutional reviews.

**Contrast / learner note.** Compatibility is often narrower and more technical; interoperability may include procedural and organisational dimensions.

**Related terms.** interface, integration, standardisation, coordination

### surveillance

**Definition.** Surveillance is the systematic observation, monitoring, or collection of information in support of situational awareness, protection, or analysis.

**Example.** The surveillance subsystem was optimised for persistent monitoring under variable lighting conditions.

**Usage note.** Use this term in capability descriptions and programme documentation where observation or monitoring functions are central.

**Contrast / learner note.** In a technical repository, focus on function and system behaviour rather than sensitive operational details.

**Related terms.** monitoring, sensing, situational awareness, observation

### sustainment

**Definition.** Sustainment is the set of activities and resources required to maintain a capability in service across its operational life.

**Example.** Early design choices improved sustainment by reducing connector variety and simplifying inspection access.

**Usage note.** Useful in lifecycle planning, support concepts, defence-oriented maintenance language, and programme affordability reviews.

**Contrast / learner note.** Sustainment is broader than maintenance because it includes logistics, training, spares, documentation, and long-term support arrangements.

**Related terms.** supportability, lifecycle, readiness, maintenance

### deployment

**Definition.** Deployment is the movement or controlled introduction of personnel, systems, or capabilities into operational use or an active field environment.

**Example.** Environmental sealing was reviewed before deployment in coastal conditions.

**Usage note.** This term often appears in planning notes, support readiness reviews, and capability summaries.

**Contrast / learner note.** In software contexts deployment may mean rollout of a build; in defence-oriented contexts it may refer to field introduction or tasking readiness. Context should clarify the meaning.

**Related terms.** fielding, rollout, operational use, readiness

### command and control

**Definition.** Command and control refers to the processes and systems used to direct, coordinate, communicate with, and supervise assets or activities.

**Example.** The system architecture included a secure interface to the command and control environment for status reporting and task updates.

**Usage note.** Use this term descriptively and institutionally, especially when discussing information flow and system integration.

**Contrast / learner note.** Avoid unnecessary abbreviation unless the audience is already familiar with the term or a glossary entry has introduced it.

**Related terms.** coordination, communication, supervision, interface

### dual-use

**Definition.** Dual-use describes technology, components, software, or knowledge that can support both civil and defence or security-related applications.

**Example.** The imaging module is considered dual-use because it supports both infrastructure inspection and resilience-oriented monitoring tasks.

**Usage note.** Very useful when discussing export review, market positioning, programme context, and institutional communication.

**Contrast / learner note.** Dual-use does not automatically imply restricted use, but it often increases the need for careful compliance and release review.

**Related terms.** controlled technology, export review, civil application, defence application

### supportability

**Definition.** Supportability is the degree to which a system design enables effective maintenance, logistics, training, documentation, and availability outcomes.

**Example.** Modular connector architecture improved supportability by reducing replacement time and simplifying stock management.

**Usage note.** Important in design trade-offs, lifecycle reviews, and maintainability-focused documentation.

**Contrast / learner note.** Supportability is broader than maintainability. A maintainable design may still be weak in supply chain or documentation support.

**Related terms.** maintainability, sustainment, readiness, spare part strategy

### hardening

**Definition.** Hardening is the process of increasing resistance to environmental stress, interference, shock, tampering, or other adverse conditions depending on context.

**Example.** Additional enclosure hardening was introduced to improve environmental resilience during transport and storage.

**Usage note.** Use cautiously and with context. The meaning may be mechanical, electronic, environmental, or cyber-related.

**Contrast / learner note.** Because the term is broad, the document should specify what kind of hardening is intended and against which condition.

**Related terms.** protection, resilience, ruggedisation, shielding

## Tone guidance for this glossary

When using defence-oriented terminology in a training repository, prefer neutral, engineering-centred language. Describe architecture, interfaces, support, readiness, and documentation logic. Avoid unnecessary operational detail. This keeps the language professional, export-aware, and appropriate for educational use.


## Writing patterns worth reusing

The value of a glossary increases when the learner converts terminology into reusable sentence patterns. After studying the entries above, build short patterns such as:

- *The system shall ... under the specified operating conditions.*
- *The inspection confirmed that ...* 
- *The interface between X and Y is responsible for ...*
- *A deviation was recorded because ...*
- *The revised document includes ...*
