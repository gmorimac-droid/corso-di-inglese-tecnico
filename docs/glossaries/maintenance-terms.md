# Maintenance Terms

!!! abstract "Scope of this glossary"
    This glossary supports communication about inspection, serviceability, failure response, corrective action, and technical support planning. It is particularly useful for learners who need to write maintenance logs, troubleshooting records, inspection notes, or service recommendations.

## How to read and reuse the entries

Each entry is written for **active professional use**. The definition gives conceptual precision, the example shows sentence-level application, and the usage note explains where the term is especially valuable in engineering communication. Where helpful, a contrast note is added to prevent common learner mistakes.

## Glossary entries

### inspection

**Definition.** Inspection is the structured examination of an item, assembly, or process in order to identify condition, conformity, wear, damage, or other relevant findings.

**Example.** Visual inspection identified contamination around the connector interface and minor deformation of the protective cover.

**Usage note.** Use *inspection* when the activity aims to observe and assess condition, whether visually, dimensionally, or through instruments.

**Contrast / learner note.** Inspection is not the same as test. A test usually evaluates function or performance; an inspection may occur without active operation.

**Related terms.** condition check, examination, conformity assessment, serviceability

### serviceability

**Definition.** Serviceability is the condition of being fit for continued use or ready to perform the intended function safely and effectively.

**Example.** The component remained serviceable despite superficial surface marks because no loss of function or structural integrity was detected.

**Usage note.** This term is valuable in maintenance findings, release-to-service decisions, and technical evaluations after inspection.

**Contrast / learner note.** Serviceability does not always mean perfect condition. It means acceptable condition for continued use within defined limits.

**Related terms.** airworthy or fit-for-service concepts, inspection result, acceptance, release

### overhaul

**Definition.** An overhaul is a major maintenance action in which an item is disassembled, inspected, restored, repaired, or replaced to recover required performance or service life.

**Example.** The gearbox was removed for overhaul after repeated vibration excursions exceeded maintenance thresholds.

**Usage note.** Use this term when the maintenance activity is extensive and planned as a substantial restoration action.

**Contrast / learner note.** Overhaul is much broader than routine servicing or minor replacement.

**Related terms.** repair, refurbishment, maintenance interval, disassembly

### replacement

**Definition.** Replacement is the removal of an existing item and the installation of another item in order to restore function, condition, or compliance.

**Example.** Sensor replacement resolved the intermittent output issue observed during calibration.

**Usage note.** Common in maintenance logs, corrective actions, and parts management.

**Contrast / learner note.** A replacement may be temporary, permanent, like-for-like, or upgraded. If that distinction matters, state it explicitly.

**Related terms.** spare part, corrective action, installation, serviceability

### scheduled maintenance

**Definition.** Scheduled maintenance is maintenance performed at predetermined intervals or according to predefined usage criteria.

**Example.** Scheduled maintenance includes filter inspection every six months and connector integrity checks at each major service interval.

**Usage note.** Useful in support planning, maintenance manuals, and lifecycle strategy discussions.

**Contrast / learner note.** Scheduled maintenance is proactive. Unscheduled maintenance is triggered by faults, damage, or unexpected findings.

**Related terms.** preventive maintenance, interval, inspection plan, support strategy

### unscheduled maintenance

**Definition.** Unscheduled maintenance is maintenance initiated by failure, degradation, unexpected behaviour, or inspection findings outside the normal planned interval.

**Example.** Unscheduled maintenance was required after the unit reported repeated communication faults during field operation.

**Usage note.** Appropriate in incident follow-up, support statistics, and reliability discussions.

**Contrast / learner note.** Frequent unscheduled maintenance may indicate design weakness, inadequate operating procedures, or environmental mismatch.

**Related terms.** corrective maintenance, downtime, failure, troubleshooting

### preventive maintenance

**Definition.** Preventive maintenance is maintenance intended to reduce the probability of failure or performance degradation before a fault becomes evident.

**Example.** Preventive maintenance actions include lubrication, seal inspection, and verification of fastening torque.

**Usage note.** Use this term when explaining planned actions designed to preserve serviceability.

**Contrast / learner note.** Preventive maintenance aims to avoid faults. Corrective maintenance responds after a problem has already emerged.

**Related terms.** scheduled maintenance, reliability, service interval, condition monitoring

### corrective action

**Definition.** Corrective action is the action taken to eliminate the cause of a detected nonconformity, fault, or undesirable condition in order to prevent recurrence.

**Example.** Corrective action included replacement of the damaged connector and revision of the cable routing instruction.

**Usage note.** This term is stronger than a simple repair because it emphasises recurrence prevention as well as immediate recovery.

**Contrast / learner note.** A temporary fix is not always corrective action if the underlying cause remains unaddressed.

**Related terms.** root cause, containment, preventive action, nonconformity

### downtime

**Definition.** Downtime is the period during which a system or item is not available for its intended use due to maintenance, failure, or other inhibiting conditions.

**Example.** Connector rework reduced downtime compared with full harness replacement.

**Usage note.** Useful in maintenance planning, support metrics, and operational impact summaries.

**Contrast / learner note.** Downtime measures loss of availability, not necessarily severity of technical damage.

**Related terms.** availability, maintenance burden, turnaround time, supportability

### spare part

**Definition.** A spare part is a replacement item held in stock or available through supply channels for maintenance purposes.

**Example.** Lead time for the spare part affected the expected restoration date for the affected unit.

**Usage note.** Important in logistics, support planning, repair decisions, and readiness discussions.

**Contrast / learner note.** Not every replacement item is immediately a stocked spare part. Availability and supply arrangements matter.

**Related terms.** lead time, replacement, stock level, supportability

### failure mode

**Definition.** A failure mode is the specific manner in which a component, subsystem, or process can fail or stop meeting its intended function.

**Example.** One identified failure mode involved intermittent signal drop caused by reduced contact pressure at the connector.

**Usage note.** Use this term in reliability analysis, troubleshooting, design review, and corrective action planning.

**Contrast / learner note.** A failure mode is the manner of failure; the root cause explains why it occurred.

**Related terms.** root cause, fault symptom, degradation mechanism, mitigation

### troubleshooting

**Definition.** Troubleshooting is the structured process of identifying, isolating, and resolving the cause of a technical problem.

**Example.** Troubleshooting began with power checks, continued with connector inspection, and concluded with replacement of the damaged interface board.

**Usage note.** This term is central in maintenance communication and diagnostic workflows.

**Contrast / learner note.** Good troubleshooting is evidence-based. Avoid presenting assumptions as confirmed causes.

**Related terms.** fault isolation, symptom, corrective action, verification test

## Preferred maintenance reporting language

- Inspection revealed no abnormal wear.
- The item remains serviceable pending routine replacement at the next scheduled interval.
- Unscheduled maintenance was initiated following repeated fault indications.
- Corrective action addressed both the damaged component and the contributing installation practice.


## Writing patterns worth reusing

The value of a glossary increases when the learner converts terminology into reusable sentence patterns. After studying the entries above, build short patterns such as:

- *The system shall ... under the specified operating conditions.*
- *The inspection confirmed that ...* 
- *The interface between X and Y is responsible for ...*
- *A deviation was recorded because ...*
- *The revised document includes ...*
