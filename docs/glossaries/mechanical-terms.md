# Mechanical Terms

!!! abstract "Scope of this glossary"
    This glossary collects terms frequently used when describing structures, moving parts, loads, tolerances, alignment, and mechanical behaviour. It is particularly useful for maintenance documentation, component description, manufacturing notes, and technical reporting involving physical assemblies.

## How to read and reuse the entries

Each entry is written for **active professional use**. The definition gives conceptual precision, the example shows sentence-level application, and the usage note explains where the term is especially valuable in engineering communication. Where helpful, a contrast note is added to prevent common learner mistakes.

## Glossary entries

### load

**Definition.** Load is the force or combination of forces applied to a component, structure, or system during operation, handling, or testing.

**Example.** The mounting frame shall withstand the specified static and dynamic load without permanent deformation.

**Usage note.** Always clarify the type of load when relevant: static, dynamic, axial, radial, thermal, or shock-related.

**Contrast / learner note.** Learners often write *pressure* when they really mean *load*. Pressure is force per unit area; load is the broader engineering concept.

**Related terms.** stress, strain, torque, fatigue

### torque

**Definition.** Torque is the turning effect produced by a force acting at a distance from an axis or pivot.

**Example.** The fastening procedure specifies the required torque to prevent loosening during vibration exposure.

**Usage note.** Common in installation instructions, maintenance tasks, and motor or transmission descriptions.

**Contrast / learner note.** Do not replace *torque* with *force*. They are related but not equivalent.

**Related terms.** rotation, shaft, fastener, alignment

### shaft

**Definition.** A shaft is a rotating mechanical element used to transmit motion, torque, or power between connected parts.

**Example.** Inspection of the drive shaft revealed surface wear near the coupling interface.

**Usage note.** Use this term in descriptions of motors, gearboxes, actuators, pumps, and rotating equipment.

**Contrast / learner note.** A shaft transmits rotation. A rod may transfer force or provide structural support but is not necessarily rotational.

**Related terms.** bearing, coupling, rotation, alignment

### bracket

**Definition.** A bracket is a structural support element used to hold, secure, or position a component relative to another structure.

**Example.** The sensor bracket was redesigned to reduce vibration-induced displacement during field trials.

**Usage note.** Useful in assembly descriptions, installation notes, and design modifications.

**Contrast / learner note.** A bracket is not just any support. In technical descriptions it usually implies a shaped mounting element with a defined fixing function.

**Related terms.** mounting, fastener, support, housing

### tolerance

**Definition.** Tolerance is the permissible variation in a dimension, parameter, or characteristic from its nominal value.

**Example.** Machining tolerance on the mating surface shall be maintained to ensure correct sealing performance.

**Usage note.** This term is essential in manufacturing, inspection, assembly fit, and specification documents.

**Contrast / learner note.** Tolerance is not the same as error. A tolerance is an allowed range; an error is an undesired deviation from a true or target value.

**Related terms.** clearance, fit, dimensional control, acceptance criteria

### fatigue

**Definition.** Fatigue is progressive material damage caused by repeated or cyclic loading, often leading to crack initiation and eventual failure.

**Example.** The revised design reduces fatigue risk at the bracket interface by lowering local stress concentration.

**Usage note.** Appropriate in durability discussions, test reports, design reviews, and failure analysis.

**Contrast / learner note.** Fatigue is not simply wear. Wear concerns material loss from contact and motion, while fatigue concerns repeated stress cycles.

**Related terms.** stress concentration, crack, endurance, failure mode

### wear

**Definition.** Wear is the gradual loss or degradation of material at contacting surfaces due to friction, motion, contamination, or repeated use.

**Example.** Excessive wear was observed on the guide surface after prolonged operation in a contaminated environment.

**Usage note.** Common in maintenance findings, inspection reports, and lifecycle evaluations.

**Contrast / learner note.** Wear may exist without immediate failure, but it can still reduce efficiency, accuracy, or service life.

**Related terms.** abrasion, friction, lubrication, serviceability

### stiffness

**Definition.** Stiffness is the resistance of a component or structure to elastic deformation under applied load.

**Example.** Additional reinforcement increased bracket stiffness and reduced unwanted movement during transport vibration.

**Usage note.** Use this word in structural behaviour discussions and comparisons between design options.

**Contrast / learner note.** Stiffness is different from strength. A component may be strong enough not to fail but still insufficiently stiff for alignment or precision requirements.

**Related terms.** deflection, strength, rigidity, deformation

### alignment

**Definition.** Alignment is the correct relative positioning of parts so that motion, load transfer, sealing, or interface behaviour remains within acceptable limits.

**Example.** Misalignment of the coupling caused abnormal vibration and accelerated bearing wear.

**Usage note.** Important in rotating equipment, mechanical interfaces, installation tasks, and troubleshooting reports.

**Contrast / learner note.** Alignment problems often create secondary symptoms such as noise, overheating, or uneven wear.

**Related terms.** fit, interface, tolerance, inspection

### seal

**Definition.** A seal is a feature or component designed to prevent leakage, contamination ingress, or unwanted transfer between adjacent areas.

**Example.** The seal must remain effective throughout the specified temperature range and pressure cycle.

**Usage note.** Useful in fluid systems, enclosures, environmental protection discussions, and maintenance notes.

**Contrast / learner note.** A seal can refer to a discrete component or to the achieved sealing function. Context should make this clear.

**Related terms.** gasket, housing, leakage, enclosure

### housing

**Definition.** A housing is the external structure or enclosure that supports, protects, or contains internal components.

**Example.** The aluminium housing provides mechanical protection and acts as a thermal path for heat dissipation.

**Usage note.** Common in product descriptions, mechanical drawings, and design summaries.

**Contrast / learner note.** Housing is broader than *cover*. A cover may close an opening, while a housing often forms the main structural body.

**Related terms.** enclosure, bracket, mounting, protection

### fastener

**Definition.** A fastener is a mechanical element used to join or secure parts together, such as a bolt, screw, nut, rivet, or clamp.

**Example.** All fasteners in the external assembly shall be secured according to the approved torque procedure.

**Usage note.** Prefer this term when describing the joining function in a general way, especially in instructions and parts lists.

**Contrast / learner note.** Do not use *screw* when the exact element is unknown. *Fastener* is safer and more professional unless the specific type matters.

**Related terms.** torque, bracket, assembly, retention

## Typical mechanical sentence patterns

- The assembly shall withstand the applied load without permanent deformation.
- Inspection revealed wear at the rotating interface.
- Misalignment may increase vibration and reduce service life.
- The housing supports the internal components and protects them from contamination.


## Writing patterns worth reusing

The value of a glossary increases when the learner converts terminology into reusable sentence patterns. After studying the entries above, build short patterns such as:

- *The system shall ... under the specified operating conditions.*
- *The inspection confirmed that ...* 
- *The interface between X and Y is responsible for ...*
- *A deviation was recorded because ...*
- *The revised document includes ...*
