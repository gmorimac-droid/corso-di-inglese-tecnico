# Methodology

## How the Course Works

This course uses a **documentation-centred methodology**.

The learner is not trained only through grammar exercises or generic writing tasks. The course focuses on the kinds of communication that appear in real technical environments, such as:
- description;
- instruction;
- reporting;
- review;
- coordination;
- controlled knowledge transfer.

The goal is to develop language that is not only correct, but also clear, structured, and professionally usable.

## At a Glance

The methodology combines four elements:

1. **Language awareness**  
   noticing vocabulary, grammar, tone, and structure;

2. **Professional modelling**  
   observing how technical documents are written and organised;

3. **Applied production**  
   producing written and spoken output with a clear purpose;

4. **Editorial revision**  
   improving the output until it becomes documentation-ready.

## Core Learning Cycle

Each module follows the same general cycle.

### 1. Input
The learner reads, observes, or analyses a model text, a phrase set, a procedure, or a technical scenario.

### 2. Analysis
Key terminology, grammar patterns, and document functions are identified and discussed.

### 3. Controlled Practice
The learner completes guided tasks with visible support and limited complexity.

### 4. Applied Production
The learner writes, reformulates, explains, or presents a technical topic more independently.

### 5. Revision and Publication
The learner revises the output and converts it into a structured Markdown page that can be included in the course repository.

## Why This Method Fits Technical English

Technical English is strongly shaped by purpose.

A sentence used in:
- a requirement,
- a maintenance log,
- an incident summary,
- or a meeting note

may use similar vocabulary, but it does not perform the same function.

For this reason, the course does not treat language as a simple list of words and rules. It teaches how language behaves inside technical documents.

## Key Distinctions the Learner Must Control

A central part of the methodology is learning to distinguish between:

- definition and instruction;
- observation and conclusion;
- requirement and recommendation;
- fault description and causal interpretation;
- neutral reporting and persuasive wording.

These distinctions are essential in professional technical communication.

## Role of Comparison and Revision

An important feature of the course is the comparison between **weak** and **strong** technical writing.

Weak writing may be grammatically possible, but still professionally inadequate. It may be:
- vague;
- repetitive;
- incomplete;
- poorly structured;
- difficult to review.

Strong writing is more:
- precise;
- explicit;
- consistent;
- useful to the reader;
- aligned with the document purpose.

For this reason, the learner is encouraged to ask not only:

> Is this sentence correct?

but also:

> Is this sentence clear, useful, and fit for technical documentation?

## Role of the Instructor or Reviewer

The instructor works less as a traditional lecturer and more as a **technical language reviewer**.

Feedback should therefore focus on:
- reducing ambiguity;
- improving terminological discipline;
- increasing documentary usefulness;
- strengthening structural logic;
- aligning wording with communicative purpose.

## Progression Through the Course

The course moves from foundational control toward documentation autonomy.

### Early Stage
Focus on:
- shared vocabulary;
- grammar control;
- reading strategies;
- basic technical phrasing.

### Middle Stage
Focus on:
- description;
- explanation;
- reporting;
- email writing;
- procedural language.

### Advanced Stage
Focus on:
- troubleshooting;
- safety communication;
- compliance language;
- defence-oriented professional language;
- documentation architecture with MkDocs and GitHub.

## Recommended Study Behaviour

The methodology works best when the learner:

- keeps a personal glossary with definitions and examples;
- revises outputs instead of accepting the first draft;
- links new content to earlier pages and terms;
- reuses templates and phrasebanks instead of starting from zero;
- treats the site as a living technical repository.

## In Practical Terms

This course is based on a simple idea:

**technical English improves faster when the learner produces usable documents, not only isolated answers.**

That is why the course treats every task as a possible contribution to a larger documentation system.
