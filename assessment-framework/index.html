    Assessment Framework¶

    Assessment philosophy¶

    Assessment in this course is designed to measure usable professional competence, not isolated language recall. The learner is evaluated on the ability to understand, produce, revise, and organise technical English in forms that resemble real engineering communication.

    For this reason, the assessment framework combines three perspectives:

    • diagnostic assessment at the beginning;
    • formative assessment during the course;
    • summative assessment through the final documentation project.

    Diagnostic assessment¶

    The diagnostic phase is intended to establish the learner’s starting profile. It typically includes:

    • a short technical reading task;
    • controlled vocabulary classification;
    • a brief descriptive writing sample;
    • a small error-correction exercise;
    • an oral explanation of a familiar technical object or process.

    The diagnostic result is not primarily a score. It is a map of strengths and weaknesses. It helps determine whether the learner needs more support in terminology, grammar, structure, register, or documentary discipline.

    Formative assessment criteria¶

    During the course, outputs are reviewed according to recurring criteria.

    1. Accuracy¶

    Does the learner use grammar, terminology, and sentence structure with sufficient control for a B2 technical context?

    2. Clarity¶

    Can the intended meaning be understood without avoidable ambiguity or unnecessary interpretation?

    3. Relevance¶

    Does the text include the information that matters for the engineering purpose of the document?

    4. Consistency¶

    Are terminology, style, units, naming conventions, and document logic stable across the text?

    5. Structure¶

    Is the information organised in a way that supports review, retrieval, and action?

    6. Professional tone¶

    Is the language appropriately neutral, precise, and audience-aware?

    Suggested scoring grid for module tasks¶

    Criterion 1 2 3 4 5
    Terminology mostly inaccurate limited control acceptable basic control good domain control strong, stable, precise use
    Grammar frequent interference noticeable weaknesses serviceable B2 control generally accurate accurate and well managed
    Clarity often unclear partially clear mostly clear clear and efficient highly clear and review-friendly
    Structure weakly organised uneven functional well organised highly disciplined
    Professional usefulness low limited usable clearly usable directly publishable

    Summative assessment¶

    The main final assessment is the documentation project. The learner is expected to assemble a coherent set of Markdown pages into a structured MkDocs site. The site should demonstrate:

    • appropriate module coverage;
    • coherent navigation;
    • documentation-quality writing;
    • evidence of revision;
    • reuse of glossaries, phrasebanks, and templates;
    • appropriate cross-linking and internal consistency.

    Weighting suggestion¶

    A practical weighting model is the following:

    • Module work and short assignments: 40%
    • Language tools and glossary development: 15%
    • Exercises and applied writing tasks: 15%
    • Final MkDocs/GitHub project: 30%

    The weighting can be adapted depending on whether the course is delivered in an academic or workplace setting.

    Feedback model¶

    Feedback is most effective when it is layered.

    1. Immediate correction for obvious linguistic issues;
    2. Explanatory feedback for pattern-level problems;
    3. Revision targets for documentary improvement;
    4. Final editorial comments on the publishable version.

    This layered model helps the learner understand not only what is wrong, but also how professional texts are progressively strengthened.
